Procedure

  1. Label one test-tube “Raw” and the other “Boiled.” Place both in the rack.
  2. Extract albumen:
    • Crack open the raw egg into a beaker and draw about 2 mL of albumen into the syringe.
    • For the boiled egg, use the scalpel to scrape off about 2 mL of the cooked albumen into a second beaker and draw up with the syringe.
  3. Dispense into test-tubes:
    • “Raw” tube: eject the 2 mL raw albumen.
    • “Boiled” tube: eject the 2 mL boiled albumen.
  4. Add reagents:
    • To each tube, add 3 mL of 1% CuSO₄ solution.
    • Then add about 1 mL of NaOH solution (or a few small pellets to dissolve).
  5. Mix gently with the glass rod. If the mixture is cloudy, you can warm gently over the Bunsen flame for 5–10 s—don’t boil.
  6. Observe colour change in each tube.

Expected Results & Interpretation

Test-tube Observation Inference
Raw Solution turns violet-pink Protein in raw albumen reacts: Biuret positive.
Boiled Fainter pink or no change Denaturation reduces peptide link reaction.

Chemical basis: Peptide bonds + Cu2+ → alkaline violet complex

How to Write It in the Exam

Aim: “To test for protein in egg albumen using the Biuret test.”

Apparatus & Reagents: List exactly the items from (i)–(ix).

Procedure: Numbered, clear, including safety (eye protection, heating tips).

Observation Table: As above.

Result & Conclusion: “Raw albumen gave pink-violet colour, indicating presence of protein. Boiled albumen gave little/no change due to denaturation.”

Calculations:

  • Procedure: Add HCl to NaHCO₃ and pass the gas through lime water
  • Observation: Effervescence; lime water turns milky
  • Inference: CO₂ gas produced

Title: Verification of Hooke’s Law using a Spiral Spring

Apparatus

  • Retort stand with clamp (3)
  • G-clamp
  • Spiral spring
  • Two 100 cm rulers
  • 100 g mass (labelled m)
  • Two pieces of thread with loops
  • Stopwatch
  • Optical pin
  • Sellotape

Aim

To verify Hooke’s Law and determine the spring constant by measuring the extension of a spring with increasing loads.

Theory

Hooke’s Law states that the extension e of a spring is directly proportional to the applied force F, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded.

F = ke

  • F = applied force (in newtons)
  • k = spring constant (N/m)
  • e = extension (in meters)

Procedure

  1. Clamp the spring vertically using a retort stand and G-clamp.
  2. Attach the pointer (optical pin) horizontally to the bottom of the spring using sellotape.
  3. Fix a meter rule vertically beside the spring to measure extension.
  4. Record the initial pointer position L0 (without any mass).
  5. Suspend the 100 g mass gently and allow it to come to rest.
  6. Record the new length L and compute extension e = L - L0.
  7. Repeat by adding more 100 g masses up to 5 or 6 steps.
  8. Record all readings in a table.
  9. Plot a graph of Force (F) against Extension (e).

Observation Table

S/N Mass (g) Force (N) Pointer Reading (cm) Extension (cm)
11000.9815.22.1
22001.9617.34.2
33002.9419.56.4
44003.9221.48.3
55004.9023.810.7

Note: Force is calculated using F = mg, with g = 9.8 m/s²

Graph

- Plot Force (N) on the vertical axis
- Plot Extension (cm) on the horizontal axis
- A straight-line graph confirms Hooke’s Law

Calculations

From the graph, determine the slope:
k = F / e
This slope gives the spring constant k in N/cm or N/m.

Conclusion

  • The graph of Force vs. Extension is a straight line.
  • Hooke’s Law is verified.
  • The spring constant k is determined from the graph.

Precautions

  • Avoid overloading the spring.
  • Take readings at eye level to avoid parallax error.
  • Ensure the pointer is horizontal.
  • Add weights gently to avoid oscillations.

Sample Viva Questions

  1. What does Hooke’s Law state?
  2. What is meant by the spring constant?
  3. Why is it important to avoid stretching the spring beyond its elastic limit?
  4. What is the function of the pointer?
  5. What kind of graph should you expect?

Apparatus

  • Rectangular glass prism (glass block)
  • Plane mirror
  • Four optical pins
  • Drawing board
  • Drawing paper
  • Thumb pins
  • Ruler and pencil
  • Protractor

Aim

To trace the path of a ray of light through a rectangular glass block and determine the angles of incidence, refraction, and emergence.

Theory

When light travels from a less dense medium (air) to a denser medium (glass), it bends towards the normal. On exiting back into air, it bends away from the normal. This is known as refraction.

Snell’s Law: n = sin(i) / sin(r)

Procedure

  1. Fix a sheet of white paper on a drawing board using thumb pins.
  2. Place the rectangular glass block in the center and trace its outline.
  3. Draw a normal on one face of the block and mark an angle of incidence (e.g. 30°).
  4. Place two pins along the incident ray.
  5. Reinsert the block, observe from the opposite side, and align two more pins such that all four pins lie in a straight line.
  6. Remove the block and draw the emergent ray.
  7. Measure and record the angles of refraction and emergence.
  8. Repeat the procedure for different values of the angle of incidence.

Observation Table

S/N Angle of Incidence (i) Angle of Refraction (r) Angle of Emergence (e)
130°19°30°
240°25°40°
350°31°50°

Graph

Plot a graph of Angle of incidence (i) vs. Angle of refraction (r).

Calculations

Apply Snell’s Law for each pair of angles: n = sin(i) / sin(r)

Conclusion

  • Light is refracted when it passes through the glass block.
  • The emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray.
  • The refractive index of the glass is calculated using Snell’s Law.

Precautions

  • Fix pins vertically and firmly.
  • Ensure accurate pin alignment to avoid parallax error.
  • Do not disturb the glass block once its outline is traced.

Sample Viva Questions

  1. What is refraction?
  2. State Snell’s Law.
  3. Why does light bend in glass?
  4. What is meant by the angle of incidence and angle of refraction?
  5. Why is the emergent ray parallel to the incident ray?

🎯 Aim

To determine the resistance of a given resistor using a potentiometer and verify Ohm’s Law.

⚙️ Theory

Ohm’s Law states: V = IR

Where:

  • V is voltage across the resistor
  • I is current through the resistor
  • R is resistance

By plotting V against I, the slope of the graph gives the resistance R.

🧰 Procedure

  1. Connect the circuit with the resistor, rheostat, ammeter, and power source in series.
  2. Connect a voltmeter across the resistor Rx.
  3. Use the jockey to tap different points on the potentiometer and record voltage readings.
  4. Adjust the rheostat to vary current.
  5. Record at least five sets of voltage and current readings.
  6. Plot a graph of V against I.

📊 Observation Table

S/N Voltage (V) Current (I) Resistance (V/I) Ω
10.100.101.00
20.080.081.00
30.060.061.00
40.040.041.00
50.020.021.00

📈 Graph

Plot a graph of Voltage (V) on the y-axis against Current (I) on the x-axis.

The slope of the graph gives the resistance (R).

📐 Calculations

From the graph: R = ΔV / ΔI

✅ Conclusion

  • The graph of V against I is a straight line through the origin.
  • This confirms that the resistor obeys Ohm’s Law.
  • The resistance of Rx is approximately 1Ω as labeled.

⚠️ Precautions

  • Ensure all connections are tight and correct.
  • Read meters at eye level to avoid parallax errors.
  • Use fresh cells to maintain constant voltage.
  • Allow the circuit to cool if resistance increases due to heating.

  2. Why is the voltmeter connected in parallel?
  3. What does the slope of the V-I graph represent?
  4. How does temperature affect resistance?
  5. What is the role of the rheostat in this experiment?
